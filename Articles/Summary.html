Summary of articles in reading list (Airbnb)

This document summarizes the (a) themes and (b) findings from articles in the reading list relating to the assignment on Airbnb.

1. Housing Affordability, Gentrification, and Urban Displacement

  • Barron, K., E. Kung, and D. Proserpio. 2018. “The Sharing Economy and Housing Affordability: Evidence from Airbnb.”

  • Horn, K., and M. Merante. 2017. “Is Home Sharing Driving up Rents? Evidence from Airbnb in Boston.” Journal of Housing Economics 38:14–24.

  • Lee, D. 2016. “How Airbnb Short-Term Rentals Exacerbate Los Angeles’s Affordable Housing Crisis: Analysis and Policy Recommendations.” Harvard Law & Policy Review 10 (1):229–54.

  • Cocola-Gant, A., and A. Gago. 2019. “Airbnb, Buy-to-Let Investment and Tourism-Driven Displacement: A Case Study in Lisbon.” Environment and Planning A: Economy and Space 0 (0):1–18.

  • Shabrina, Z., E. Arcaute, and M. Batty. 2019. “Airbnb’s Disruption of the Housing Structure in London.” ArXiv Prepring. University College London.

  • Gurran, N., and P. Phibbs. 2017. “When Tourists Move in: How Should Urban Planners Respond to Airbnb?” Journal of the American Planning Association 83 (1):80–92.

  • Ferreri, Mara, and Romola Sanyal. 2018. “Platform Economies and Urban Planning: Airbnb and Regulated Deregulation in London.” Urban Studies 55 (15):3353–68.

Study Content & Methodology Key Points / Findings
Barron, Kung & Proserpio (2018) US cities, 2008–2016. Econometric panel models exploiting Airbnb listings growth and housing supply constraints.

  • Airbnb increases rents by 0.4–1% per 10% increase in listings. Effect stronger in central/supply-constrained neighbourhoods.

  • Method for computing listings = (a) host join date, (b) 3 months after host join dates and after every guest review, (c) 6 months after host join dates and after every guest review

  • Data supplemented with Google Trends data for search term “airbnb”.

  • Two channels: (a) inducing landlords to switch from supplying long-term rentals to short-term rentals, (b) enables homeowners to generate income from excess housing capacity which raises the value of owning relative to renting, increases price-to-rent ratio.

  • Recommends approaches to limit the reallocation of housing stock from long-term to short-term rentals, without discouraging use of home-sharing. Occupancy tax or proof of owner-occupancy requirements.
Horn & Merante (2017) Boston, 2012–2015. Difference-in-differences analysis with census tracts.

  • One SD increase in Airbnb listings associated with increase in rents of 0.4% in Boston.

  • One SD increase in Airbnb listings relative to total housing units correlated with 5.9% decrease in rental units offered for rent.

  • 82% of hosts had only one simultaneous listing on Airbnb in Boston, suggesting that most Airbnb hosts are occupants seeking extra income by renting. However, only 18% had multiple properties, they represented almost half of those listed on Airbnb, suggesting that a large proportion were leased by commercial operators

  • Useful in breaking down data from Airbnb by (i) density, (ii) listing types – Table 4 and 5.

  • Controls for (i) crime rates as proxy for popularity, (ii) building permits issued as proxy for physical improvements, (iii) restaurant licenses as proxy for amenities.

  • Home sharing both a personal and commercial enterprise - should be regulated as such.
Lee (2016) Los Angeles. Legal and policy analysis, review of Airbnb listings and city regulations.

  • Airbnb distorts the housing market in two ways (a) conversion – unit previously occupied by city resident but now listed is unit removed from rental market, typically in core regions, reducing housing supply and affordable housing supply (b) hotelisation – incentive to list units for tourists over locals due to ‘rent-controlled rent’.

  • Results in bidding up of rents as public sector cannot swifty replace the housing units that Airbnb removes from the marketplace.

  • Particularly affects high density neighbourhoods, tight housing markets with near-zero vacancy rates, and could be exacerbated by annual increases in residential demand.

  • Leads to citywide reduction in affordable housing – given that affordable units are particularly attractive for conversion, reducing stock. Incentivizes the direct conversion of subsidised or rent-controlled units into lucrative Airbnb listings.

  • Other phenomena such as displacement, gentrification through rising rents, exacerbates social inequality through displacement of lower income tenants.

  • Policy recommendations: limit entire-home rentals, require registration, enforce data transparency, integrate short-term rental impacts into city planning.
Cocola-Gant & Gago (2019) Lisbon, Portugal. Case study using interviews, local housing data, and Airbnb spatial analysis.

  • Airbnb encourages a process of buy-to-led investment where different players make profit from rents and displace residents with tourists.

  • Airbnb acts as instrument that contributes to financialization of housing – short-term rentals offer numerous benefits that encourage market efficiency for property owners, making them increasingly attractive for local and global investors. Investors enjoy steady rental yield and can sell asset tenant free at any moment.

  • Airbnb gives rise to a hyper-flexible rental market that amplifies insecurity and displacement concerns.

  • Buy-to-let gentrification experienced by residents as process of social injustice.

  • Data from Airdna.co – 76% of listings were entire homes. Focused on case study area of Alfama near Lisbon.
Shabrina, Arcaute & Batty (2019)

London, 2015–2018. Spatial analysis using geocoded Airbnb listings and housing data.

Housing supply data from Valuation Office Agency (VOA) which counts number of houses in each LSOA.

LSOA type of homeownership data from UK Census 2011, which provides summary of areas with proportion of each type of homeownership.

  • Spatial location of Airbnb rentals is negatively correlated with diversity of dwelling types and positively correlated with a single dwelling type – purpose-built flats, conversions and flats in commercial buildings.

  • Airbnb concentrated in areas with high proportion of privately rented properties, detracting more than 14% of housing supply to short-term rentals, and up to 20% in some neighbourhoods.

  • Highlights the unique context of London’s housing market (a) prolonged decline in new homes constructed since 1970s, leading to housing shortfall, and supply constraints. (b) scarcity of developable land in London (c) regulatory constraints and housing policies that seeks to target first-time buyers pushes prices up further due to increase in demand that outstrips supply (d) London is centre of speculative market with overseas landlords and buy-to-let landlords who buy without intention to live in property (e) rise of private companies that manage airbnb properties in exchange for profit share

  • 69,000 observations in London in June 2018, from which 50,000 (72%) have at least one review and can be considered as active. 55% are listings of entire rooms, 44% are private rooms, and 1% correspond to shared rooms.

  • Previous studies have found: (a) turnover of establishments are high, 49% exited after registering, (b) non-professional hosts (one listing instead of multiple listings) are 13.6% more likely to exit the market (c) caveat that difficult to quantify the overall evolution and growth of airbnb but shows how listings have cumulatively increased over the years.
Gurran & Phibbs (2017)

Data from insideabnb.com across Sydney metropolitan area. Number of listings by geographic area, per host, nightly prices and reviews

Census data on median monthly mortgages and state government data on median monthly rents

  • Airbnb triggers “touristification” of residential neighborhoods.

  • Nearly 1% of total dwellings and 3.26% of total rental stock were available for short-term stay by Airbnb. Nearly 1/3 of greater Sydney’s listings were held by individuals who own multiple properties.

  • Number of dwellings removed from the permanent rental market about half of current rental vacancy. Popular tourist areas nearer to the city centre had more than three times the number of whole units compared to the vacancy rate.

  • Creates trade-offs between tourism support and housing affordability.

  • Urban planners should integrate short-term rental impacts into zoning and housing policies.
Ferreri & Sanyal (2018) London. Qualitative analysis of policy documents, Airbnb data, and interviews.

  • London exhibits “regulated deregulation”: Airbnb operates legally in practice with selective enforcement.

  • Gap between regulation and enforcement: even where rules exist (zoning, planning permission, restrictions on short-term lets), local authorities struggle to access the data sets held by platforms, monitor and enforce compliance. This creates “informal” or semi-informal spaces of platform use outside full regulatory control.

  • Shift toward “regulated deregulation” means that planning regimes become more flexible (or weaker) in response to platform pressures, raising questions about housing affordability, access, equity and the traditional urban planning remit.

  • Highlights need for coordinated urban governance to mitigate housing pressures.
Bivens (2019) US-wide. Literature synthesis and policy assessment.

  • Economic costs of Airbnb likely outweigh the benefits. costs to renters and local jurisdictions likely exceed the benefits to travellers and property owners.

  • Evidence that Airbnb increases the supply of short-term travel accommodations and slightly lower prices. But there is little evidence that the high price of travel accommodations is a pressing economic problem in the United States:

  • Instead, rising housing costs are a key problem for American families, and evidence suggests that the presence of Airbnb raises local housing costs.

  • Benefits:

    (1) Property owners can diversify into short-term rentals and increase return on assets. However, housing wealth is primarily concentrated among the top 20%.

    (2) Increased options and price competition for travellers accommodations. However, this is in effect a redistribution of welfare, and not an increase in overall economy wide welfare. Little effect on developers building more accommodation in the city but instead results in a conversion of long-term rental units into short-term rental units.

    (3) Increased tourism spending and economic prospects of cities. However, relies on assumption that all spending done by travellers staying at Airbnb properties is spending that would not have been done had Airbnb not existed.

  • Costs:

    (1) Long-term renters face higher housing costs.

    (2) Local government tax collections fall

    (3) Externalities inflicted on neighbours

  • Policy interventions recommended to limit negative effects while retaining some economic benefits.

3. Data, transparency and effectiveness of research methods

  • Alsudais, Abdulkareem. 2021. “Incorrect Data in the Widely Used Inside Airbnb Dataset.” Decision Support Systems 141:113453.

Study Content & Methodology Key Points / Findings
Alsudais et al. (2021) Data audit of Inside Airbnb dataset.

  • Identified significant errors: mismatched coordinates, duplicates, missing values

  • Warns that many empirical studies using this data may be biased.

  • Highlights the need for careful data cleaning and validation.
Cos et al. (2016) New York City. Analysis of Airbnb-released data and media reporting.

  • Airbnb manipulated data to under report commercial listings.

  • Casual home-sharing overstated relative to professional hosts.

  • Platform opacity protects corporate interests and complicates policy making

Study Content Key Points / Findings
Brockes, E. 2023 Reflective commentary on the cultural and economic evolution of Airbnb, drawing from lived experience and housing trends.
  • Airbnb began as a peer-to-peer sharing ideal but evolved into a profit-driven system dominated by professional hosts. It contributed to rent increases and displacement, losing its “sharing” ethos.
Clark, J. 2023. Investigative feature on UK’s rental crisis through tenant and agent interviews.

  • Chronic shortage of rental housing, intensified competition, and Airbnb conversions contributing to scarcity.

  • Reflects wider affordability issues in London and other cities.
Harris, J. 2018. Opinion piece contextualising Airbnb within wider gentrification and commodification debates.

  • Airbnb accelerates displacement and hollowing out of communities.

  • Calls for regulatory intervention and community-based housing policy responses.
Minton, A. 2023 Opinion piece linking NYC’s regulatory crackdown on short-term lets to global urban housing debates.

  • Stronger regulation (e.g., NYC’s Local Law 18) can rebalance housing markets.

  • Encourages cities like London to follow with strict registration and enforcement.
Jolly, J. 2023 Data-driven investigation into foreign property ownership and opacity of UK land registries.
  • Limited transparency in property ownership exacerbates housing inequality and speculative investment pressures in cities.
Neate, R. 2023 Journalistic piece on hyper-luxury property markets.

  • Illustrates extreme wealth concentration and its spatial manifestation in London’s housing landscape.

  • Contextualises inequality alongside Airbnb-driven affordability issues.
Iqbal, N., and A. Chakrabortty. 2023 Explores population shifts in London due to rising housing costs and the hollowing out of family-friendly housing.

  • Short-term lets and speculative ownership reduce long-term residents, leading to falling school enrolment.

  • Highlights social impacts of housing market dynamics.
Wall, Tom (2024) Investigative report based on council data and market analysis.
  • Warns that proposed deregulation could worsen rental scarcity; estimates over 10,000 London homes at risk of conversion to holiday lets.
Jones, Rupert (2024) Explainer summarising government consultation and public responses on short-term let restrictions.
  • Emphasises need for local control over conversions; identifies trade-off between tourism and housing supply.
